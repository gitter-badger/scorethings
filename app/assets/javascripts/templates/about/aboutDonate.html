I picked two Seattle based Science, Technology, Engineering and Math (STEM) programs because I like to write code, and I think it's important to help expose tech to people who would normally think that they couldn't have a career in a STEM career field.

Check out the following cool non-profit organizations helping change STEM education.

Technology Access Foundation (TAF) - helps kids learn STEM

Website: http://techaccess.org/
Follow @stembytaf

The following is totally my opinion, not TAF's.

TAF if a nonprofit organization that works in Science, Technology, Engineering and Math (STEM), helping kids in "communities of color and those with low income". I'm not going to quote the entire website to you. I attended their after school program in high school in, I think 1999. It was in a small office space next to a Taco Del Mar. After seeing their website, I'm really happy to see that they've grown so much to help so many other kids.

The founder, Trish Millines Dziko, told us she started the program when she was working at Microsoft during the early years. She wanted to see more minorities in the tech industry, so she did something about it, and started TAF with other volunteers.

Honestly, I wasn't that into computers at the time in high school, and I didn't really understand why she and other volunteers worked so hard. I kind of get it now. I'm pretty sure there are a whole lot of (way more talented) workers in the tech industry today that are also grateful for TAF.

If you care about technology and diversity in the tech sector, donate to them, follow them on Twitter.

Ada Developers Academy (Ada) - intensive software developer training school for women

Website: http://adadevelopersacademy.org/
Follow @adaacademy

The following is totally my opinion, not Ada Academy's.

I'm gonna be kind of rude and say I didn't know about Ada Developers Academy (Ada), not like I did TAF. I've heard about cool projects that the students had presented at meetups, and I really like the idea of all the hard work that goes into teaching the students about agile development (scrum, pair programming, test driven development, etc.). That's something you can't learn from a screencast or book.

Plus, I heard from someone about how it's really challenging, but you can produce actual open source projects by the time you finish the program, and intern at some of the top tech companies. I totally think you should donate to their program, which helps students with the cost of the program.

Plus, they put their curriculum and projects on GitHub: https://github.com/Ada-Developers-Academy
